It's a letter of introduction - employer's first impression of you. It can be difficult to replace the first impression. Important and worthwhile to craft the cover letter. Story beginning / middle / end. You will get your resume read that way.
What makes a good cover letter: strong letters engage reader and are tailored for the specific business applied for. Not one size fits all. Otherwise lose impact.

Choose language carefully, keep professional. Brief summary of exp and qualifications, highlight key strengths/achievements making you the right pick. Describe why you're interested, including qualifications , outline exp ideal candidate for position, outline relevant edu/exp, and reflect enthusiasm and willingness to learn. Helps hiring manager: what value/benefit does this person bring to my org. A good cover letter is a mini work sample - test for pro writing, ability to convey ideas concisely following a formal structure. Attention to detail thru proc.

Read the job application instructions very carefully before writing cover letter and applying. Quickest in waste basket = ignore application. Look whether attach / email / snail mail in resume/cover letter. Look for specific info to include such as salary reqs, work exp, and skill/skillset. Often times the employers see the application / posting as the first test. They want to see you can read / follow instructions. If you mail in resume or decide to apply online, they may not consider bc you didn't pass that first test.


Key elems to writing good cover letter:
1 - No spelling grammar or typing errors whatsoever. This is generally a display of your best efforts. If you aren't attentive to detail, you might lose points in that process. Ensure you proof everything or ask someone. 

2 - address it specifically if possible to the person that can hire you. Resumes sent to personnel dept can have rougher time going to the right person. If you network / research exactly who's making the hiring decision, address letter to that person. Ensure name spelled correctly, title correct. We have caution about using gender specific titles in address (sometimes you won't know) which they would use. Dear Firstname Lastname is better than Mr/Miss/Ms etc, it might be less awkward. Some names could be either and there's a lot of traditional male names like Shaun that now females use that as well. You don't always know, ere on side of caution. If applying for things where Dr. that is making the decision, identify them by Dr. bc important to them. If can't find specific person, always to Dear Hiring Manager / Personnel / Human Resources. Not to whom it may concern, old school times is not desirable anymore / advantageous. 

3 - write in your own words. Make it sound like you, authentic, you want to show them that you know something about their company and industry. 

4 - Research comes into play. Know the company, what they do, try to convey you didn't just pick them out or random google search and find out about them. Convey you know who they are / what they do and you have chosen them. 

5-  use terms and phrases relevant, including reqs from job posting. Best keywords / buzzwords will come from there. You'll have info or network with people in the industry, applying for position just reference the posting in the ad. Duties, what the company does, and the requirements / qualifications. Those are the things to encourage to look at first. See whether it's in your ballpark, would you be interested in using these skills / qualifications. Do you have enough of those to actually get the job. When you decide this is a fit, read full thing and then apply. Match qualifications and use the word for them so whether applicant tracking system or HR screener they'll know you meet the things you're looking for easily without having to figure out whether using same vocab - just use their wording.

Types of cover letters:
application, prospecting, networking, value proposition, referral, cold contact, and email. 
Two common: cover letter for job posting response, and a cold cover letter. More commonly is the first one - in response to specific job posting. In this case it's important to keep job posting specifics in mind. Travel / relocation / salary. Tailor and target CL to specific job requirements. Try and tell them your availability in your response. Only address salary prefs if they request it. Try to phrase it in a range so you don't limit opportunities. In regard to request for salary, I am seeking a position which pays from high 40k to low 50k depending on overall benefits and responsibilities that come with the position. You can always say I'm willing to discuss a salary that's commensurate based on my experience and education in an interview. You don't want to ask they asked for that info so you have to respond. 

The other thing to remember = respond to job posting quickly. Faster = more attention it may receive. Follow those instructions closely.

Cold cover letter - getting foot in the door. With cold, you can directly contact potential employers without employer, previous corerspondence, or a job posting. Most will do at some point to reach out who reasonably could be expected to have suitable job openings.

Structure of a cover letter. If printed, begin with traditional business letter format. 
Name, address, etc. Avoid abbreviations in the address except for the state "NY" and "PO box". 
Name / address / city state zip / phone number / email / / Date written in full MMMM-DD-YYYY / / Name of Recipient / TItle of Recipient / Company / Address / City State Zip.

For CL in address of email, remove addr info from top of letter. Includ your full contact info after your signature instead. Respectfully, Sincerely, your name, contact info in your signature box. Moved from the top of email to the bottom. 
Avoid gentlemen / dear sirs outdated language.

First para answers why am I writing. Grab attention, get interested, customise CL to the job. Identify position and company (where you got it from Indeed/linkedin etc). Try to be creative / enthusiastic if possible. 

Second para is why am I the best fit. Grabbed attn so demo why you're fit. Match qualifications to the job. Try not to list every skill you've had. Highlight what are most relevant to that job opening and company and reader. Explain what you have to offer to the employer. Look at their news, about us, mission statement. Employer is looking for a solution. Problem solver, can help with challenges, qualities most employers look for.

Final para to seal the deal, ask for an interview. Thank employer for time in reviewing qualifications, restate desire to work for a company. Ask for action/response from employer, or advise followup plans and timeframe. Closing short and simple. Final words professional. Close with Sincerely / Respectfully.

T-Format CL. Comparing constrasting what employer is looking for vs what you have. Stack job requirements on the left. On the right, explaining how you directly meet those. You don't want to come up with a bunch, but pick the best / relevant to give idea of how you meet those qualifications. 
Sometimes setting up T format can be challenging. People may not be familiar for lining up perfectly. You can also do it easier. The point is sometimes similar skills / not exact. It can give more of a narrative, same exact thing, similar skills etc, what it led me to the same end. It just can help them see you're a better fit. List out qualifications, can help with recognizing those words. 
Saw people using this during great recession, businesses were not hiring. Laying off people, unemployment was high. To stand out from traditional, use these because look a little diff, formatted diff. More interesting read for employers. Can always try it. Sometimes these are helpful in career transition - get creative by explaining you have similar skills vs not exactly but you know you can do the job.
